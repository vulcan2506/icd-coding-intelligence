# Outpatient First-Listed Diagnosis Coding
*Source: ICD-10-CM FY25 Guidelines October 1, 2024.pdf*

**Feature:** Outpatient First-Listed Diagnosis Coding

**Key Behaviors:**
- In the outpatient setting, the term first-listed diagnosis is used in lieu of principal diagnosis.
- The coding conventions of ICD-10-CM and general/disease-specific guidelines take precedence over outpatient guidelines.
- The search for the correct code assignment must begin through the Alphabetic Index, never the Tabular List initially.
- For diagnostic services only, sequence the diagnosis chiefly responsible for services provided during the encounter first.
- For interpreted diagnostic tests with available final reports, code confirmed or definitive diagnoses and do not code related signs and symptoms as additional diagnoses.

**Requirements / Properties:**
- Diagnoses may not be established at the time of the initial encounter and may require two or more visits for confirmation.
- Codes for other diagnoses, such as chronic conditions, may be sequenced as additional diagnoses after the first-listed.
- Routine laboratory/radiology testing without signs, symptoms, or associated diagnosis requires assignment of Z01.89.
- If routine testing occurs during the same encounter as a non-routine test evaluating a sign, symptom, or diagnosis, both the Z code and the reason code must be assigned.
- Final reports for diagnostic tests must be available at the time of coding to assign confirmed or definitive diagnoses.

**Deprecated in this version:**
- Beginning the search for code assignment in the Tabular List initially.
- Coding related signs and symptoms as additional diagnoses when a confirmed or definitive diagnosis is documented in an interpreted test report.
- Using the term 'principal diagnosis' in the outpatient setting.

**New in this version:**
- Explicit instruction to use 'first-listed diagnosis' terminology instead of 'principal diagnosis' for outpatient claims.
- Mandatory sequencing of Z01.89 for routine laboratory/radiology testing in the absence of other clinical indicators.
- Requirement to assign both Z codes and specific reason codes when routine and non-routine testing occur in the same encounter.
