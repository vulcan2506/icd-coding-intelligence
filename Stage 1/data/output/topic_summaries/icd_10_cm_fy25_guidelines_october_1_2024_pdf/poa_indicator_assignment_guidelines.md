# POA Indicator Assignment Guidelines
*Source: ICD-10-CM FY25 Guidelines October 1, 2024.pdf*

**Feature:** POA Indicator Assignment and Exemptions

**Key Behaviors:**
- The POA indicator is assigned to diagnosis and external cause codes after ICD-10-CM codes are assigned in accordance with official coding guidelines.
- Medical record documentation from any provider legally accountable for establishing the patient's diagnosis may be used to support the determination of POA status.
- Providers must be queried regarding issues related to the linking of signs/symptoms, timing of test results, and the timing of findings.
- The guidelines supplement but do not replace the main body of ICD-10-CM Official Guidelines for Coding and Reporting.
- A joint effort between the healthcare provider and the coder is essential to achieve complete and accurate documentation and reporting.

**Requirements / Properties:**
- The provider must be legally accountable for establishing the patient's diagnosis to support the determination of whether a condition was present on admission.
- The POA indicator must be assigned to conditions that have been coded in accordance with Sections I, II, and III of the official coding guidelines.
- Detailed documentation is required to determine if a condition was present on admission versus acquired during the healthcare encounter.
- External cause of injury codes must also have a POA indicator assigned unless exempted.
- The determination of POA status relies on clinical judgment and documentation from the provider involved in the care and treatment of the patient.
- The term 'provider' is explicitly defined as a physician or any qualified healthcare practitioner who is legally accountable for establishing the patient's diagnosis.
- The CDC website must be consulted to obtain the detailed list of ICD-10-CM codes exempt from POA indicator usage.

**Deprecated in this version:**
- Using the POA guidelines to determine when a condition should be coded rather than applying them to the final set of assigned codes.
- Relying solely on the POA guidelines without referencing the main body of ICD-10-CM Official Guidelines for Coding and Reporting.
- Assigning POA indicators without considering the joint effort between the healthcare provider and the coder.
- Ignoring the CDC website list of ICD-10-CM codes that do not require the use of a POA indicator.
- Failing to query the provider regarding the timing of findings when documentation is unclear.

**New in this version:**
- The specific requirement to use the CDC website for the detailed list of ICD-10-CM codes exempt from POA indicator usage.
- The explicit inclusion of external cause of injury codes in the scope of POA indicator assignment.
- The mandate that medical record documentation from any provider involved in the care and treatment of the patient may be used to support POA determination.
- The clarification that the term 'provider' includes any qualified healthcare practitioner legally accountable for establishing the patient's diagnosis.
- The emphasis on querying providers regarding the linking of signs/symptoms and timing of test results as part of the POA determination process.
